# rebuttal-soups-round-2

We thank the reviewers for their feedback. We address the main concerns below.

1. **Novelty**
   * On RA’s concerns about engaging with findings reported in prior work: Sections 4.2.4 and 4.2.5 may read as contradicting prior IT-OT work, when our primary intent was to extend it. Gallardo et al. [16] address pre-incident risk assessment; our study focuses on post-incident response. We will add this distinction. We do provide evidence against the recommendations that prior work has articulated, namely to argue for increased OT-IT convergence and collaboration. Counterintuitively, the professionals we interviewed articulated a different approach, namely decomposition, which makes sense given that incident response is highly time-compressed and coordination channels are highly constrained, making direct coordination and shared situational awareness very difficult. That said, we did not aim to suggest that this strategy is also correct for other security-relevant functions. We will carefully frame our reasoning more narrowly here, in line with the evidence.
   * We thank RC for the references on OT and safety science; engaging with this literature, including Ebert et al. (2023) and Gnanasekaran et al. (2025), will strengthen our framing in Section 5.1. 

2. **Generalizability**
   * We will more explicitly address the generalizability of our findings. European TSOs share grid codes under ENTSO-E, common regulatory frameworks, and cross-border coordination obligations during incident response, making the organizational logic we observed unlikely to be highly specific to the TSO we studied. Furthermore, in line with [a], we argue that single-case studies produce transferable insights when underlying mechanisms are explained rather than merely described.
   * [a] March et al.(1991), Learning from Samples of One or Fewer. Organization Science 2(1):1-13. 
   * We also thank RC for suggesting to add more organizational context to make the findings transferable to other critical infrastructures. While maintaining the organization's confidentiality, we will add to Section 3.1 a brief description of the TSO's regulatory setting and operational scope. 

3. **Scenarios and methodology**
   * On ecological validity (RB): Root cause was a deliberate part of scenario design. Our worry was that if we left out the root cause, this would hamper the respondents ability to think through how incident response would unfold. This indeed imposes a limitation: it removes the uncertainty from the scenario and thus the process of root cause analysis. 
   * All eight participants engaged with all four scenarios (RB). Seven had no preference and started with S1. P1 preferred to start with S3. 
   * RC asked about our choice of individual interviews versus focus groups: within the scope of this study, we asked each practitioner to envision how they and other units would respond. A focus group would have encouraged consensus and obscured the individual unit perspectives we needed. We will add this rationale to Section 3.1 and recognize focus group methodology as a direction for future work in Section 5.3.

4. **Supporting the findings**
   * We acknowledge that reading our Results section might give an impression of reporting individual quotes without showing how they emerge from the coding. We will add to each Results subsection a sentence identifying the codebook code (Appendix B) that drives the theme. 
   * To address RD's concern about specificity, we will clarify our framing in Section 5.2 that cybersecurity is not perceived in this organization as a separate threat requiring distinct capabilities, but as an extension of the generalized incident response capabilities already in place. These capabilities are indeed documented in a formal incident response procedure, as RA is asking.

5. **Further clarifications and corrections**
   * We thank RB for flagging grammatical errors and RA for highlighting where terms need to be clarified. We’ll add a URL for [10], spell out HREC (Human Research Ethics Committee) and clarify in Section 4.3.3 that the liaison officer's interface is with OT frontline practitioners, and other clarifications. 
   * We will also implement the suggestions of RA: information sharing as cross-unit information passing (4.1.2); role descriptions per unit (Table 1); point of detection and decision as temporal moments (4.2.1); categorization driven by "does this affect grid operations" (4.2.2); external institutions specified as government authorities, DSOs, and infrastructure providers (4.3.4); joint exercises as pre-incident capability building (4.4.2).
