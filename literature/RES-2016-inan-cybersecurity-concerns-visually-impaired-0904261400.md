## Research Summary [p. 28]

### WHY — The Gap This Study Addresses

At the time of publication, Internet use among people with visual impairments was substantially lower than in the general population, pointing to a persistent digital divide. Prior research documented what visually impaired users *did* online but said almost nothing about their cybersecurity experiences, concerns, or capabilities. The specific intersection of visual impairment and cybersecurity was therefore an underexplored gap. The study was motivated by two linked concerns: first, that limited accessibility of security features (e.g., CAPTCHAs, misleading links, missing alt text) puts visually impaired users at disproportionate risk; and second, that cybersecurity anxiety might itself be causing these users to *reduce* their Internet use, thereby deepening the digital divide rather than bridging it.

### HOW — Methodology

The study used a structured survey administered face-to-face by trained research assistants to twenty adults with visual impairments recruited from a special-purpose school and an adult rehabilitation centre in Texas. Questions were read aloud and answers recorded verbatim, making the data collection fully accessible. The survey covered four domains: demographics and background; technology and Internet use; cybersecurity threats and concerns; and cybersecurity knowledge, attitudes, beliefs, and confidence. Correlations between these domains were analysed using Pearson correlation coefficients to test for patterns between Internet behaviour and cybersecurity capability.

### WHAT — Key Results

Participants with higher cybersecurity knowledge and skills were *more* concerned about cybersecurity, not less — and used the Internet *less* frequently. This counterintuitive finding suggests that awareness of threats leads to avoidance behaviour, which risks widening the digital divide. The most feared concerns were identity theft, financial information access, and privacy exposure. The most commonly experienced Internet problems were inaccessible design issues (missing alt text, complex forms, auto-refreshing pages) followed by misleading links and CAPTCHAs. Email and general browsing remained the dominant activities, but educational use of the Internet was more prevalent than prior studies had found — a positive shift likely driven by growth in online learning platforms.

## Abstract [p. 28]

Twenty individuals with visual impairments were surveyed in order to (a) understand their Internet use and (b) examine relations between metrics related to Internet use and cybersecurity-related knowledge, skills, confidence, and attitudes. Participants used the Internet for various purposes, including information search, communication, chatting, shopping, socialisation, and education. The latter was more prevalent than in past research. Participants who were more knowledgeable and skilled regarding cybersecurity tended to be more concerned about it and to use the Internet less than those who were less knowledgeable about cybersecurity. Thus, cybersecurity concerns may lead individuals with visual impairments to decrease their Internet use, which could widen the digital divide.

**Keywords:** Accessibility; Internet use; Individuals with visual impairments; Disabilities; Cybersecurity

## 1. Introduction [p. 28]

According to the 2012 National Health Interview Survey, about nine percent (21.5 million) of the adult population in the US had some kind of visual impairment. The American Printing House for the Blind reported that there were over 500,000 children who were blind in the United States. In addition to the congenitally blind population, the number of people with adventitious blindness — people who have lost their vision later in life — is also significant. The Blinded Veterans Association reports that around 7,000 veterans in the United States are vulnerable to losing their vision and becoming blind or visually impaired each year, in addition to about 165,000 who are already blind or visually impaired. According to the 2012 Disability Status Report, about 24.6% of people who are blind or visually impaired are employed full-time.

Given that most corporations conduct their daily business affairs online, it is vital to educate people who are visually impaired in the use of computers at home and/or work.

The Internet and assistive technology can help people with disabilities maximise their potential and achieve personal, professional, and educational objectives. These technologies allow them to access information, work independently, execute errands such as shopping, participate in education and training, and communicate and socialise with others. There are various challenges that Internet users who are visually impaired experience when surfing the Web, including cybersecurity concerns and accessibility issues. Even though a great number of preventative measures have been taken to make the Internet more accessible, users with visual impairments still may have safety concerns when navigating the Internet. Fear of cyber-attacks and exposure of sensitive information, as well as limited accessibility of the Internet and its features, hinder the benefit that users with visual impairments may enjoy.

Cyber-attacks are the primary concern threatening US infrastructure, the economy, and civilians' safety. The Internet Security Threat Report published by Symantec Inc. shows an overall increase in targeted attacks and an increase in the number of breaches in 2014. Over a million identities were exposed, and about 38% of mobile users experienced mobile cybercrime in the same year. The estimated annual cost of cybercrime to the global economy is more than 400 billion dollars. The increasing number of cybercrime incidents raises the need for better protection for individuals, and in particular those with visual impairments.

### Internet Use by Individuals Who Are Visually Impaired [p. 29]

Individuals with a disability could utilise various Internet services for banking, shopping, training, and social networking. Previous studies indicated that Internet use for communication (e.g., email) was the most commonly observed activity among individuals with visual impairments. Surfing the Internet for fun, listening to music, and social media involvement were among the other most frequently identified activities. Although it was not common, the use of the Internet for banking, shopping, and educational purposes was also found to be substantial. However, findings from previous studies suggest that people with visual impairments tend to use computers and the Internet at rates below the average for the general population, indicating that a major digital divide still exists for users who are visually impaired. A US Department of Commerce report found that Internet use among households with people with a disability is only 42.6%, compared to 77.7% for households with no disability. Similarly, a recent Oxford Internet Institute report from the United Kingdom found that only 48% of people with a disability use the Internet, compared to 83% of non-disabled respondents.

To allow easy access to the Internet, there are a number of free web browsers specifically designed for users who are blind. The use of assistive technology integrated with these web browsers enables Internet users who are visually impaired to navigate and access the Web more easily. In addition to accessible web browsers, a number of free and commercial screen readers are also available to users with a visual disability. Although screen readers enable accessibility to content, they may not always be usable. The top five causes of frustration faced by users when using screen readers are: (a) a confusing layout, (b) technical conflicts between the screen readers and applications, (c) poorly designed forms, (d) no alternate text for images, and (e) inaccessibility to PDF files. Most screen readers have other limitations, such as text-based presentation, linear access to information, inaccessibility of visual objects, loss of contexts, and repetition of reading.

Internet users who are visually impaired are more vulnerable to cyber-attacks due to the absence or limitation of visual cues, inaccessibility of visual cues, and lack of software support to inform the users about the potential cybersecurity threats. In addition to challenges due to the design of web pages, there are other security challenges that users who are blind confront when surfing the Web, including the use of CAPTCHA, login sessions and timeouts, security updates, malware, and phishing.

### Definitions [p. 29–30]

**Cybersecurity Threats:** Any potentially harmful processes and actions performed to (1) access and use private information (e.g., identity theft); (2) attempt to deceive and scam users (e.g., spam emails); (3) install software intended to perform an unauthorised process (e.g., viruses and malware); or (4) directly attack computer systems and networks (e.g., hacking).

**Individuals with Visual Impairments:** Individuals who are functionally blind or have low vision, who may use screen reader programmes to carry out computing tasks.

**Screen Readers:** Specially designed software programmes which convert text displayed on the computing device screen to audible text.

**Cybersecurity Skills:** Individuals' ability to perform required tasks to secure computing devices and to manage cybersecurity threats.

**Cybersecurity Confidence:** Individuals' perceptions about their own skills, knowledge, and capabilities to accomplish specific tasks to deal with cybersecurity issues.

**Cybersecurity Knowledge:** Individuals' awareness and understanding of cybersecurity issues and risks associated with cyber threats.

**Attitudes Toward Cybersecurity Threats:** Individuals' intrinsic feelings of distress and discomfort due to cybersecurity concerns.

### Purpose of the Study [p. 30]

This manuscript reports the results of a survey conducted with twenty individuals who are visually impaired. The purpose of this study was to investigate the use of the Internet by individuals who are visually impaired and explore their cybersecurity challenges and concerns while surfing the Internet. Findings will help identify the major concerns and challenges of this group of Internet users and determine their assistive technology needs.

More specifically, the following research questions were addressed:

1. What types of assistive technologies and software tools are used by individuals with visual impairments?
2. What types of Internet and social media activities are carried out by individuals with visual impairments?
3. What types of cybersecurity concerns do individuals with visual impairments have while browsing the Internet?
4. Is there any common pattern between the types of Internet activities, cybersecurity concerns, and individuals' cybersecurity knowledge, skills, confidence, and attitudes?

## 2. Methodology [p. 30]

### Participants [p. 30]

The participants were twenty individuals with visual impairments over the age of 18. They were recruited through a special-purpose school serving students who are blind or visually impaired (n = 10) and an adult rehabilitation centre (n = 10) in Texas. The questionnaires were directly administered to each participant by a trained research assistant. The majority of participants were individuals who were blind, and about half of the participants were female (50%). The range of participants' ages was between 18 and 77 years old. Most participants (80%) were employed. In terms of ethnicity, the largest portion of the participants was white (60%).

### Data Collection Instruments [p. 31]

A survey investigating the cybersecurity concerns of individuals with visual impairments was developed and used for data collection. The survey consisted of four major sections: (1) demographics and background; (2) technology/Internet use; (3) cybersecurity threats and concerns; and (4) cybersecurity knowledge, attitudes, beliefs, and confidence. The fourth section was made up of four sub-scales. The majority of the items were created by a team of researchers including experts from special education, instructional technology, psychology, and computer science. In order to build an accessible instrument, survey design guidelines for persons who are visually impaired proposed by Kaczmirek and Wolff (2007) were followed.

### Procedures [p. 31]

The data collection procedures began once potential participants were identified through professional networking and other recruitment methods. Participants were contacted via telephone or email and a meeting time and location was scheduled. Participants either read a consent form prior to meeting or had the form read to them before the interview, at which point the participant signed the consent form. Interview questions were read aloud and multiple-choice answers were marked by the research assistant/interviewer. The open-ended questions were read aloud and the participants' answers were written verbatim. Following each interview, the interviewer input the answers into an online survey form submitted to the researchers at Texas Tech University.

### Cybersecurity Knowledge, Skills, Confidence, and Attitudes [p. 36]

An examination of participants' cybersecurity knowledge, skills, confidence, and attitudes indicated that the participants had moderate mean scores on these constructs, ranging from 2.97 to 3.47. Pearson correlation coefficients were computed to investigate whether any correlation existed between these factors and the level of cybersecurity concern, frequency of internet activities, social media involvement, and frequency of internet problems/issues.

Participants' knowledge and skills had statistically significant negative correlations with level of cybersecurity concern (r = −.61, p < .01 and r = −.55, p < .05) and frequency of Internet activities (r = −.47, p < .05 and r = −.45, p < .05). On the other hand, frequency of Internet issues/problems was found to have a positive and statistically significant correlation with the frequency of internet activities (r = .53, p < .05) and social media involvement (r = .55, p < .05).

## 4. Discussion [p. 36–37]

The purpose of this study was to explore Internet use by individuals with visual impairments and their concerns toward cybersecurity threats and relations between cybersecurity concerns and frequency of Internet activities.

In terms of Internet use, the results showed that various activities were being used by the individuals. Among those, email communication and browsing the Internet for fun/information were the most frequently reported activities — consistent with prior research. However, this study also revealed an increased use of the Internet for education purposes, probably due to the movement to provide more accessible educational opportunities via online platforms. Social networking and listening to music, as well as activities such as banking and shopping, were also commonly reported.

In regard to Internet accessibility problems and security concerns, the results of this study revealed that critical issues were mainly related to the design of websites — missing alternate text, complex forms, auto-refreshing pages. The navigation structure and website organisation were the commonly reported difficulties due to the lack of accessible design and the overwhelming amount of information presented on web pages. Such difficulties force individuals with visual impairments to spend excessive time to complete their intended tasks individually, or to rely on other people for acquiring information.

Examination of user concerns showed that the majority of cybersecurity threats received a serious concern rating. Among those, the possibility of someone tracking their Internet activities was the highest rated concern. These concerns related to security and privacy of personal information may keep many from engaging in online social activities and becoming online consumers.

Examination of participants' data revealed that individuals have a moderate level of knowledge, confidence, and skill in cybersecurity. Furthermore, participants' knowledge and skills were found to be related to their level of cybersecurity concerns, suggesting that when participants have a high level of cybersecurity knowledge and skills, their concerns were also high toward cybersecurity threats. Additionally, there were relationships between participants' knowledge, skills, confidence, and frequency of Internet activities; however, the direction of the relationship was negative, suggesting that individuals with higher knowledge, skills, and confidence less frequently use the Internet. This result may be due to users having changed their behaviour because of security concerns, as previous research found that users with security concerns are less likely to disclose personal information, buy goods, and bank online.

---

## 5. Conclusions and Future Directions [p. 37]

Future research should include the exploration and development of additional cybersecurity warnings for individuals who are blind or visually impaired to increase their degree of confidence in using the Internet. Collaborative research that includes software and commonly used browser developers would enhance cybersecurity for individuals who are visually impaired on a broader basis if done from inception rather than after the fact. Including the end users with the disability in any such research is imperative.

Additional studies should focus on a user-centric view of concerns associated with cybersecurity and should explore how these vulnerable users are reacting or responding to threats. The ongoing challenge for all accessibility issues is keeping up with the ever-changing technology and threats found on the Internet, but exploration of how to increase confidence of safety while using the Internet for individuals with visual impairment is greatly needed to provide equal access for this population.
